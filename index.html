Activity 1: Rooftop Solar PV System (5 kW)

System Architecture

System Specifications

  • Panel Configuration: 15 panels Ã— 335W each
  • Battery Capacity: 10 kWh with 90% DoD
  • Daily Energy Generation: ~20-25 kWh
  • Backup Duration: 6-8 hours during outages

System Explanation

This residential rooftop installation harnesses sunlight through photovoltaic panels mounted on the roof structure. The solar panels convert photons into direct current electricity, which flows to an MPPT charge controller that optimizes power extraction while managing battery charging cycles. The system incorporates a lithium-ion battery bank providing energy storage for nighttime usage and grid outages. A bidirectional hybrid inverter serves dual functions: converting DC power to AC for household appliances and enabling grid interaction. During peak generation periods, excess electricity feeds back into the utility grid through net metering, earning credits for the homeowner. When solar production is insufficient, the system automatically draws power from stored energy or the grid. This configuration ensures continuous electricity supply while maximizing self-consumption and reducing electricity bills. The setup is particularly valuable in areas with frequent power cuts or high electricity tariffs, providing both energy independence and long-term cost savings through reduced utility dependence.

Activity 2: Hybrid Solar-Wind System for Rural Clinic

Hybrid System Design

Critical Loads Priority

  • Vaccine refrigeration: 24/7 operation
  • Medical diagnostic equipment
  • Emergency lighting and communication
  • Estimated daily consumption: 15-18 kWh

System Explanation

This hybrid renewable setup combines complementary energy sources to ensure reliable power for critical healthcare services in off-grid locations. Solar panels generate electricity during daylight hours, while the wind turbine operates continuously when wind speeds exceed threshold levels, typically higher during evening hours when solar output diminishes. Both energy sources feed into dedicated charge controllers that prevent battery overcharging and optimize energy capture. The battery bank stores surplus energy, providing power during periods when neither solar nor wind resources are available. A pure sine wave inverter converts stored DC electricity into clean AC power suitable for sensitive medical equipment like vaccine refrigerators and diagnostic machines. This complementary generation pattern significantly improves system reliability compared to single-source installations. Rural health clinics require uninterrupted electricity for refrigerating medicines, operating diagnostic tools, and maintaining emergency lighting. The hybrid approach minimizes reliance on diesel generators, reducing fuel costs and carbon emissions while ensuring consistent healthcare delivery in remote communities lacking grid infrastructure.

Activity 3: Community Microgrid with Multiple Sources

Microgrid Architecture

Microgrid Features

  • Total Generation Capacity: 100 kW peak
  • Community Size: 50 households + institutions
  • Biomass Fuel: Agricultural waste gasification
  • Autonomous operation with demand response

System Explanation

This comprehensive microgrid architecture integrates three distinct renewable sources to power an entire remote community with stable electricity access. The solar farm provides daytime baseload generation, while wind turbines contribute variable power based on meteorological conditions. A biomass gasification plant offers dispatchable generation using locally available agricultural residues like rice husks or wood chips, converting organic matter into combustible syngas for electricity production. An intelligent microgrid controller orchestrates energy flow between sources, optimizing generation dispatch based on availability and demand patterns. The battery system smooths intermittent renewable output and stores excess energy for peak demand periods. All components connect through a three-phase AC distribution bus that supplies residential homes, educational institutions, and community facilities. This integrated approach addresses energy poverty in isolated areas where grid extension is economically unfeasible. The biomass component provides baseload stability while utilizing local waste streams, creating circular economy benefits. Communities gain reliable electricity for productive activities, education, and quality of life improvements while developing local technical capacity for system maintenance and operation.
